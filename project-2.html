Problem Statement

Why do discounts hurt?

Most sales teams assume more discounting drives more revenue. This project proves the opposite — using 4 years of Superstore transaction data to show that orders with 40%+ discounts average -$108 profit per line, while orders with no discount average +$67.

Beyond discounting, the analysis surfaces which regions, categories, and customer segments are actually driving profitability — giving sales leadership the visibility they need to make smarter decisions.

Key Findings

What the data revealed

12.5%
Overall Profit Margin

On $2.3M revenue, the business generates $286,397 profit — a healthy overall margin, but unevenly distributed across categories and regions.

-$99K
High Discount Loss

Orders with 40%+ discounts collectively generate -$99,559 in losses. Meanwhile, no-discount orders generate $329,988 profit — 3× more effective.

-$17K
Tables Sub-Category

Tables is the worst-performing sub-category at -$17,725 profit despite $206K in sales — a clear pricing and discount problem that needs immediate attention.

50.6%
Consumer Segment

The Consumer segment drives over half of total sales at $1.16M — making it the core revenue engine and highest-priority segment for retention strategies.

$725K
West Region Leads

West region dominates with $725,458 in sales and the highest profit. Central region is the weakest — Furniture is actually loss-making there at -$2,871.

Q4 📈
Consistent Seasonal Peak

Q4 is the strongest quarter every year without exception — a clear seasonal pattern that should drive inventory planning, staffing, and marketing spend.

Reflection

What I learned

The most surprising finding was how clearly the discount analysis exposed a business problem hiding in plain sight. Discounting felt like a sales driver — but the data showed it was quietly destroying margins. This is exactly the kind of insight that separates a data analyst from someone who just builds charts.

Building the Region × Category matrix on Page 3 was a Power BI technique I hadn't used before — it compresses what would normally be 12 separate charts into a single scannable table that executives can act on immediately.

The monthly trend with an average reference line was another deliberate choice — it lets stakeholders instantly see which months are above or below baseline without needing to mentally calculate averages themselves.
